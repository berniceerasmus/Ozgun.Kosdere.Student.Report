Key Weaknesses

04

Article System (a / the / ø)

Turkish has no articles, so Özgün omits or misuses them almost universally. This is his most consistent and noticeable grammar error in both spoken and written output.

Discourse Connectivity

Sentences are produced in isolation without linking devices. He rarely uses "however," "as a result," "in addition to," or "given that" — which makes his speech feel choppy and his arguments hard to follow.

Conditional Structures

He confuses zero, first, and second conditionals. In business contexts this is critical — the difference between "if we delay, the client will leave" and "if we had planned better, this wouldn't happen" carries significant meaning.

Vocabulary Range (Collocations)

Overreliance on basic verbs (do, make, have, get) instead of precise collocations: "make a decision" → fine, but misses "reach a consensus," "set a deadline," "flag a risk," "raise a concern."

Diplomatic & Polite Register

Özgün tends toward direct, assertive language that can seem blunt or even rude in international professional settings. He lacks the modal softeners and hedging language needed for polite disagreement and negotiation.

Giving Updates

"We're currently at around 70% completion on the installation work."

Disagreeing Politely

Persuading

"If we invest in this now, we'll avoid much larger costs down the line."
"The data clearly shows that this approach delivers the best results."
"I strongly believe this is the right direction for the team — and here's why."
"Would you be open to considering an alternative approach that might address your concerns?"

Leading Meetings

"Right, let's get started. The main items on today's agenda are..."

"Let's park that for now and come back to it at the end."

The Project Delay — Informing a Romanian Colleague

ROLEPLAY 01
ContextA critical installation project in Romania is 10 days behind schedule due to a supplier failure. Özgün must inform his Romanian counterpart and propose a recovery plan.
Student RoleProject Manager (Özgün) — must explain the delay, take responsibility, and propose solutions without damaging the relationship.
AI / Teacher RoleRomanian Site Manager (Mirela) — initially frustrated; open to solutions if approached professionally.
ObjectivePractice: giving bad news diplomatically, using conditionals for solutions, and managing cross-cultural professional tension.

Step-by-Step Conversation Flow

MEHMET
Good morning, Mirela. Thank you for making time. I wanted to speak with you directly about the Phase 2 installation. I'm afraid I have some difficult news to share.
MIRELA
Good morning, Özgün. That sounds concerning. What's happened?
MEHMET
Unfortunately, our main equipment supplier has informed us of a production delay. As a result, the delivery has been pushed back by approximately ten days. I understand this is not ideal, and I want to be transparent with you about the situation.
MIRELA
Ten days? Özgün, this is a serious problem. We have commitments to the client. What are you proposing to do about this?
MEHMET
I fully understand your concern, and I take responsibility for not anticipating this risk earlier. I've already been in contact with two alternative suppliers. If we approve the sourcing change today, we can recover approximately six of the ten days. I'd like to walk you through our revised plan.
MIRELA
What's the cost impact of switching suppliers at this stage?
MEHMET
There would be an additional cost of roughly €4,000. However, if we don't act now, the delay penalty from the client would be significantly higher. I believe this is the most cost-effective path forward.
MIRELA
I see. I appreciate you coming to me with a solution rather than just the problem. Let me review the supplier options and get back to you by end of day.

Useful Phrases for Özgün

"I'm afraid I have some difficult news..." "I want to be transparent with you..." "I take responsibility for..." "If we approve this today, we can recover..." "I believe this is the most cost-effective path..."

Error Correction System

10
A. Common Errors Table — From Transcript
Incorrect (Transcript) Correct Form Explanation
"We have problem with delivery." "We have a problem with the delivery." Missing article a (indefinite, singular countable noun) and the (specific delivery already in context). Turkish has no articles — high-frequency omission error.
"I make meeting with Romania team." "I had a meeting with the Romanian team." Wrong verb: make → have/had (fixed collocation: have a meeting). Missing article. Romania used as adjective → should be Romanian.
"The engineer, he told me..." "The engineer told me..." Subject pronoun doubling — common in Turkish (pro-drop language). The engineer he = redundant repetition of subject. Drop the pronoun.
"If we will finish on time, client is happy." "If we finish on time, the client will be happy." First conditional error: the if-clause uses present simple (not future). Will belongs in the result clause. Also: missing article the.
"I am work in this company since 8 years." "I have been working at this company for 8 years." Three errors: (1) Wrong tense — present perfect continuous for ongoing action. (2) in → at for companies. (3) since → for (since = point in time; for = duration).
"We need to do a decision quickly." "We need to make a decision quickly." Wrong verb in collocation: do a decision is not standard English. The fixed collocation is make a decision. High frequency business vocabulary error.
"The budget is not enough for do this." "The budget isn't sufficient to do this." Infinitive error: after prepositions and verbs like enough, use to + infinitive, not bare infinitive. Also: sufficient is more professional than enough in formal contexts.
"Yesterday I go to office and see problem." "Yesterday I went to the office and saw the problem." Simple past required for completed past actions — present tense used in error. Also: missing articles the before both office (specific, known) and problem (previously referenced).
B. Pattern Analysis

Pattern 1 — The Article System (Most Frequent Error)

Turkish has no grammatical article system (no equivalent of a, an, the). As a result, Özgün systematically omits articles in all positions — before singular countable nouns, before specific or previously mentioned referents, and in fixed expressions. This is not random but deeply systematic. Correction requires conscious rule application before automaticity develops.

Pattern 2 — Subject Pronoun Doubling

Turkish allows sentences without explicit subjects (pro-drop language). When Özgün uses a noun subject, he sometimes adds a redundant pronoun: "The manager, he said..." This is a direct transfer of a Turkish syntactic pattern. It sounds informal or non-native to English listeners and should be systematically eliminated.

Pattern 3 — Tense Conflation (Present / Past / Perfect)

Özgün regularly uses present simple where past simple or present perfect is required. This is partly due to Turkish tense system differences (Turkish aspect is marked differently). The present perfect / simple past distinction is particularly problematic and requires focused practise with timeline-based activities.

Pattern 4 — Do/Make Confusion & Weak Verb Collocations

Özgün relies heavily on the verbs do, make, have, and get where more precise collocations are required. This is a B1 plateau feature common across many L1 backgrounds. Targeted vocabulary instruction focusing on verb–noun pairs (e.g., reach a decision, set a deadline, raise a concern) will raise his register to B2 level rapidly.

C. Correction Strategy — Teacher Instructions

When to Interrupt

Do NOT interrupt mid-sentence unless the error blocks meaning entirely. Allow Özgün to complete his thought, then model the correction calmly. Constant interruption kills fluency confidence. Reserve immediate corrections for article errors (his most frequent pattern) in controlled grammar exercises only.

How to Correct

Use recasting: repeat his sentence back with the correction embedded naturally, then ask a follow-up question. This exposes the correct form without making the correction feel punitive. Example — Student: "We make meeting." Teacher: "You had a meeting — good. What was the main outcome of the meeting?"

Error Logging System

Keep a live error log during speaking tasks. At the end of 10 minutes of speaking, present the top 3 errors on paper and ask Özgün to self-correct before you provide the answer. This metacognitive step builds long-term accuracy far more effectively than immediate correction alone.

Building Accuracy

Alternate fluency tasks (uninterrupted) with accuracy tasks (controlled grammar drilling). Use the ratio 60% fluency / 40% accuracy at B1+. As patterns stabilise, shift to 70/30. Track article usage as a specific KPI — set a goal of fewer than 3 article errors per 5 minutes of speech by Month 3.
