Section 1 — The Meeting Cycle

Understand the three stages every professional meeting has.

FOUNDATION ▼

THREE STAGES — ONE PROFESSIONAL MEETING

A professional meeting is not only the conversation in the room. It has three stages, and a manager is responsible for all three.

BEFORE → prepare the agenda, share it with participants, set the time
DURING → open, guide, summarise, assign action points, close
AFTER → write and send the minutes, follow up on actions
Most people only think about "during". A manager thinks about all three.
📄 Agenda
The list of topics to discuss, sent before the meeting. It gives structure and sets expectations.
📝 Minutes
The written record of what was discussed and decided. Sent after the meeting.
✅ Action point
A specific task assigned to a person with a deadline. "What, who, when."
📬 Follow-up
The email or message sent after the meeting to confirm decisions and remind people of their actions.
🗓️ AOB
Any Other Business — a space at the end of the agenda for topics not on the original list.
🔁 Standing item
A topic that appears on every meeting agenda because it is reviewed regularly.
Manager insight: sending a clear agenda before the meeting saves time. Sending clear minutes after the meeting prevents confusion. Both show you are organised and in control.
🗓️

Section 2 — Writing a Professional Agenda

Structure and language for a clear, well-organised agenda.

BEFORE THE MEETING ▼

WHAT MAKES A GOOD AGENDA?

A good agenda has five elements: purpose, participants, time allocation per item, the person responsible for each item, and space for AOB. Each item should be a short noun phrase, not a vague heading.

vague — not useful
1. Production
2. Suppliers
3. Other things
clear — professional
1. Production delay — causes and impact (10 min)
2. Supplier performance review (15 min)
3. AOB (5 min)

AGENDA EMAIL LANGUAGE

When you send the agenda, use a short, professional email. The language should be clear and direct.

Please find the agenda attached.
Standard opening for an agenda email. Formal and clear.
The meeting will cover the following items.
Use to introduce the agenda list in the email body.
Please come prepared to discuss...
Ask participants to prepare specific information before arriving.
If you have any items to add, please let me know by...
Give a deadline for adding agenda items — prevents last-minute surprises.
The meeting is scheduled for [time] in [location].
Always confirm logistics clearly — time, date, room or link.
Please confirm your attendance by [date].
Request confirmation so you know who is coming.
Agenda email
Dear team, please find the agenda for Thursday's production meeting attached. The meeting will cover the following items: (1) delivery delays — causes and impact, (2) supplier performance review, (3) packaging process update, and (4) AOB. Please come prepared to share your team's latest output data. If you have any items to add, please let me know by Wednesday noon. The meeting is scheduled for 10:00 in Meeting Room B. Please confirm your attendance.
ETİ tip: even a short internal meeting benefits from a 3-line agenda. It shows you have a plan and helps people prepare. A meeting without an agenda often runs over time and ends without clear decisions.

Section 3 — Opening, Guiding and Closing the Meeting

Lead every stage of the meeting with confident, clear language.

DURING THE MEETING ▼

THE MEETING HAS FOUR MOVES

Every well-run meeting has the same four language moves: open → guide → summarise → close. Each move has its own set of phrases. Learn the move, not just the phrase.

Opening the meeting

Okay everyone, let's get started.

Thank you all for coming. Today's agenda covers three main items.

Let's begin with item one — the delivery delays.

B2 — more formal opening

Good morning. I'd like to call the meeting to order.

Before we start, I'd like to confirm that we have a quorum. (formal — for official meetings)

Our aim today is to reach a decision on the supplier issue.

Guiding the discussion

Let's stay focused on this point for now.

Let's move on to the next item.

We can come back to that later.

Let me finish, please.

B2 — managing time and flow

We're running short on time, so let's keep this brief.

I'd like to park that idea and return to it under AOB.

Can we table that discussion for the next meeting?

I'll give the floor to [name] now.

Summarising a point

So to summarise what we've agreed...

Let me confirm what we've decided.

The main point here is that...

B2 — checking understanding

Can I just check that we're all aligned on this?

Does everyone agree that the action here is to...?

I want to make sure this is noted before we move on.

Closing the meeting

Let's wrap up. We've covered all the agenda items.

Thank you everyone. I'll send the minutes shortly.

Our next meeting is on [date].

B2 — formal close

Before we close, let me run through the action points.

I'll circulate the minutes within 24 hours for your review.

Is there any other business before we adjourn?

Özgün
Good morning everyone. Let's get started — I know we're all busy. Today's agenda has three items: the supplier delay, packaging output, and a brief update on next week's targets. Let's begin with the supplier delay.
Colleague
Can we also talk about the quality issue from last week?
Özgün
Good point. I'll add that under AOB at the end. Let's stay focused on the supplier issue first.
— 25 minutes later —
Özgün
Before we close, let me run through the action points. [Name] will contact the supplier by Thursday. [Name] will share the packaging report by Friday noon. I'll circulate the minutes today. Any other business? No? Thank you all.
Key language pattern — giving the floor: when you invite someone to speak, use: "I'd like to give the floor to [name]" or "[Name], would you like to take us through the figures?" This sounds much more professional than just pointing or saying "you, speak."

Section 4 — Action Points: Assigning and Confirming

The most important skill — who does what, by when.

ACTION LANGUAGE ▼

THE ACTION POINT FORMULA

Every action point must answer three questions: What needs to be done? Who is responsible? When is the deadline? If any one of these is missing, the action will probably not happen.

WHAT → specific task description
WHO → one named person (not "the team")
WHEN → a specific date or time (not "soon")
✗ "Someone will check the supplier." (no name, no date)
✓ "Ahmet will contact the supplier by Thursday 5 p.m."

LANGUAGE FOR ASSIGNING ACTIONS

Use these patterns in the meeting to assign tasks clearly and professionally.

B1 — direct and clear

[Name], can you send the report by Friday?

[Name], please contact the supplier this week.

Who can take this action?

Let's agree: [Name] will do this by [date].

B2 — more formal assignment

I'd like to assign this to [Name]. [Name], can you confirm you're happy with the deadline of [date]?

The action here is for [Name] to review the data and report back by [date].

Can I put [Name] down for this?

CONFIRMING AND CHECKING ACTIONS

At the end of the meeting — or at the start of the next one — always read back and confirm all action points.

Let me run through the actions.
Use at the end of the meeting before you close. Goes through each action point aloud.
Can I just confirm who is doing what?
Use to check clarity before the meeting ends. Avoids confusion later.
Are you comfortable with that deadline?
Always check that the person agrees — this creates commitment.
I'll note that as an action for [Name].
Use when taking minutes — confirms the action is being recorded.
We agreed that [Name] would... by [date].
Use in follow-up emails to confirm what was agreed in the meeting.
Could you please update me on the status by [date]?
Use in follow-up to check progress before the deadline.
weak — no clarity
Okay, someone will look at the supplier problem soon.
strong — clear action
Ahmet, can you contact the supplier and get a confirmed delivery date by Thursday 5 p.m.?
Manager habit: before you close every meeting, say "Let me run through the actions." Then read each one aloud. This takes two minutes and prevents weeks of confusion.

Section 5 — Writing Clear Minutes

Record what happened so everyone is aligned — even those who missed the meeting.

AFTER THE MEETING ▼

WHAT DO MINUTES CONTAIN?

Minutes are not a word-for-word transcript. They are a clear, concise summary of: who attended, what was discussed, what was decided, and what the action points are.

Header: Meeting title · Date · Location · Attendees
Body: One paragraph or bullet per agenda item: what was discussed, what was decided
Footer: Action point table — What / Who / By when
Minutes should be sent within 24 hours of the meeting while everything is still fresh.

LANGUAGE FOR MINUTES — PAST TENSE, THIRD PERSON

Minutes are written in the past tense and in the third person. You report what happened — you do not address the reader directly.

B1 — simple but correct

The team discussed the supplier delay.

It was agreed that Ahmet would contact the supplier.

The next meeting will be on [date].

B2 — formal minutes language

Özgün reported that production output had fallen by 12% due to the supplier delay.

After discussion, it was agreed that the supplier contract would be reviewed.

The meeting was adjourned at 11:45.

wrong — direct address
We talked about the delay. You need to contact the supplier.
correct — reported style
The delay was discussed. It was agreed that Ahmet would contact the supplier by Thursday.
Minutes example
Meeting: Weekly Production Review · Date: 14 April · Attendees: Özgün K., Ahmet Y., Fatma D.

1. Supplier delay — Özgün reported that the main supplier had failed to deliver on time for the third consecutive week. The team discussed the impact on production targets. It was agreed that Ahmet would contact the supplier to obtain a written delivery commitment by Thursday 17 April.

2. Packaging output — Fatma presented the weekly packaging figures. Output had improved by 8% compared to the previous week. No further action required at this stage.

Action points:
• Contact supplier for written delivery confirmation — Ahmet Y. — 17 April
• Share updated packaging report — Fatma D. — 18 April noon
Common mistake: writing minutes in the present tense ("The team discusses...") or in the first person ("We talked about..."). Always use past tense and third person in minutes.

Section 6 — Follow-Up Emails and Chasing Actions

Professional language to confirm, remind, and escalate after the meeting.

FOLLOW-UP LANGUAGE ▼

THREE TYPES OF FOLLOW-UP

After a meeting, a manager sends three kinds of follow-up communication: (1) the minutes email with action points, (2) a reminder before the deadline, and (3) an escalation if the action was not completed.

Sending the minutes

Please find the minutes of today's meeting attached.

As agreed, [Name] will [action] by [date].

Please let me know if anything needs to be corrected.

Our next meeting is scheduled for [date].

B2 — minutes email opening

I am writing to share the minutes of this morning's production meeting for your records.

Please review the action points below and confirm you are on track.

If you have any corrections or additions, please respond by [date].

Chasing / reminding

Just a reminder that the report is due tomorrow.

Could you give me a quick update on this?

Have you had a chance to contact the supplier?

B2 — professional reminder

I wanted to follow up on the action from our meeting on [date].

As the deadline is approaching, could you please confirm the status?

I appreciate you are busy — please let me know if you need any support to complete this.

Escalating — when not done

This hasn't been done yet — can you update me?

We need this done urgently. Can we discuss?

B2 — formal escalation

I note that the agreed action from [date] has not yet been completed.

As this is now affecting the production schedule, I would like to discuss next steps.

Could we arrange a brief call today to resolve this?

Reminder email
Hi Ahmet, I wanted to follow up on the action from Monday's meeting. The deadline for contacting the supplier is tomorrow at 5 p.m. Could you please confirm the status? If you need any support, let me know and I'll help. Thanks — Özgün
too aggressive
Why haven't you done this yet? This is urgent.
firm but professional
I note this action is still outstanding. As it is now affecting our schedule, I'd like to discuss how we can resolve this today.
Tone note: a follow-up email should always be polite but firm. Aggressive language damages the relationship; vague language means nothing happens. The B2 patterns above give you the balance — professional pressure without conflict.

B multiple choice
1) You want to move to the next agenda item. Which is most professional?
Correct — "Let's move on to the next item" is clear, professional, and natural for a manager.
2) A colleague raises a topic that is not on the agenda. What do you say?
Exactly — you acknowledge the idea and redirect it to AOB. This is firm but respectful.
3) Which action point is written correctly?
Correct — it has all three elements: what (contact supplier, confirm date), who (Ahmet), and when (Thursday 5 p.m.).
4) Which is correct minutes language?
Exactly — minutes use past tense and third person: "it was agreed that [Name] would..."
C speaking tasks

Roleplay 1 — Open and Set the Agenda

Open a meeting about a packaging delay. Your agenda has three items: (1) the cause of the delay, (2) the impact on targets, and (3) action points. Use the four-move structure: open → set agenda → invite contribution → begin item one.

Roleplay 2 — Assign Action Points

The meeting is ending. You need to assign two actions: one to a colleague for contacting a supplier, and one to yourself for preparing a report. Use the What / Who / When formula. Ask each person to confirm the deadline.

Roleplay 3 — Write a Chase Email

It is the day before a deadline. A colleague has not updated you on their action point. Write a short, professional reminder email (4–5 lines). Be polite, specific, and firm. Include the original action, the deadline, and a request for a status update.

Teacher tip: for Roleplay 2, interrupt Özgün once when he is assigning the action — say "I'm quite busy this week, is Thursday okay?" — and see if he handles the negotiation professionally. He should acknowledge, ask about capacity, and still confirm a specific deadline.
