Section 1 — The Manager’s Job in a Meeting

Understand what leadership language sounds like in English.

CORE SKILL ▼

MANAGER LANGUAGE ≠ LONG LANGUAGE

A good manager does not speak more. A good manager speaks more clearly. In English, strong management language is often short, direct, calm, and organised.

Open the meeting → Set the focus → Guide the discussion → Close with action
Example pattern: "Let’s get started. Today I’d like to focus on..."
too weak / unclear
Maybe we can talk about some problems and then maybe decide something.
manager style
Let’s focus on the delay first, then agree on the next step.
Key idea: Leadership language gives direction. It helps people know what the meeting is about, what matters most, and what happens next.

Section 2 — Taking Control of the Meeting

Open, direct, refocus, and move the discussion forward.

PHRASES ▼

USEFUL PHRASE FAMILIES

Managers often use short functional phrases. Learn the function, not only the sentence.

Let’s get started.
Use it to open the meeting confidently.
Today I’d like to focus on...
Use it to set the agenda.
Let’s stay focused on...
Use it when the discussion goes off-topic.
Let’s move on to the next point.
Use it to manage time and keep control.
We can come back to that later.
Use it to postpone a topic politely.
Let me finish, please.
Use it to handle interruption professionally.
Özgün
Okay everyone, let’s get started. Today I’d like to focus on three things: delivery delays, supplier communication, and next week’s production targets.
Colleague
Can we also talk about packaging issues?
Özgün
Yes, good point. Let’s cover that at the end if we have time.
Manager move: first acknowledge the idea, then redirect the meeting. This sounds firm but respectful.

Section 3 — Explaining Your Decision Clearly

Structure your explanation so people can follow you.

STRUCTURE ▼

THE 3-STEP EXPLANATION

When you explain a decision, use a simple structure: reason → impact → next step.

The main reason is...
This is causing...
So what we need to do is...
This pattern makes you sound organised and professional.
Özgün
The main reason for this change is efficiency. At the moment, we lose too much time during the packaging stage. This is causing delays in delivery. So what we need to do is review the process and simplify the handover between teams.

Useful starters

The main issue is...
The problem is...
What we need to do is...
The next step is...

Useful endings

...to avoid further delay.
...so we can work more efficiently.
...before the next production cycle.

⚖️

Section 4 — Respectfully Disagreeing

Disagree without sounding rude, defensive, or too soft.

HIGH VALUE ▼

ACKNOWLEDGE → DISAGREE → REASON

This is the safest and most professional pattern for disagreement in meetings.

I understand your point, but I think...
I see what you mean, however the main issue is...
That’s a fair point, but we also need to consider...
too direct
No, that’s wrong.
professional
I understand your point, but I’m not sure that’s the best option at this stage.
Colleague
We should change the supplier immediately.
Özgün
I understand the concern, but I don’t think changing the supplier now is the best solution. It may create more disruption in the short term.
Important: In English, disagreement often sounds stronger than you expect. Small phrases like I think, I’m not sure, and at this stage help soften your message.

Section 6 — Roleplay Tasks

Use the target language in realistic management situations.

SPEAKING ▼

Roleplay 1 — First Meeting as a New Manager

  • Start the meeting.
  • Set two priorities.
  • Redirect one off-topic comment.
  • Close with one action point.

Roleplay 2 — Strong Disagreement in the Team

  • A colleague says the supplier must change immediately.
  • You disagree.
  • Use: acknowledge → disagree → reason.

Roleplay 3 — Explain a Process Change

  • Explain why the packaging process needs to change.
  • Say what problem it causes now.
  • Say what the team should do next.
Teacher tip: interrupt him during roleplay once or twice. He should practise recovering control with phrases like Let me finish, please and I’ll come back to that in a moment.
