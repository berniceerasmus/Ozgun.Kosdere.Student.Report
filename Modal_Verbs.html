Grammar — The Six Modals You Need

Obligation, advice, ability, possibility, and permission

10 MIN▼

// THE GOLDEN RULE — ALL MODALS

All modal verbs follow the same structure: modal + base verb. Never add -s, -ed, or -ing to the verb after a modal.

✗ He must to submit the report.  →  no "to" after modal
✗ She should submitting the report.  →  no -ing after modal
✗ We can't approves the budget.  →  no -s after modal
✓ He must submit the report.
✓ She should submit the report.
✓ We can't approve the budget.
ModalMeaningExample — Özgün's Context
mustStrong obligation — rule or lawAll purchases must be approved by the director.
have toExternal obligation — required by situationI have to present the Q2 report by Friday.
shouldAdvice — the right or best thing to doYou should contact the supplier before escalating.
couldSuggestion / past ability / polite requestWe could postpone the procurement to avoid the price increase.
might / mayPossibility — not certainThe delivery might be delayed because of customs.
can / can'tAbility or permission (positive/negative)I can coordinate the Romania visit. We can't change the scope now.

// MUST vs HAVE TO vs SHOULD — THE MANAGER'S CHOICE

As a manager, choosing the right modal changes your relationship with your team. Know the difference:

MUST → you are making the rule (strong authority)
✓ All engineers must submit weekly reports. (YOUR rule)
HAVE TO → the rule comes from outside (policy, system)
✓ We have to follow the procurement procedure. (company policy)
SHOULD → advice, recommendation (not a rule)
✓ You should prepare the presentation in English. (suggestion)
COULD → polite suggestion, alternative option
✓ We could also try a different supplier. (one option among many)
Power tip: Using "should" and "could" makes you sound collaborative and open-minded. Overusing "must" can sound aggressive. Good managers mix all modals depending on the situation.

Grammar 2 — Negatives, Questions & Degree

Don't have to vs must not · Polite questions · Certainty levels

7 MIN▼

// MUST NOT vs DON'T HAVE TO — CRITICAL DIFFERENCE

MUST NOT = forbidden, prohibited
✓ You must not share project costs with external parties. (absolutely forbidden)
DON'T HAVE TO = not necessary, optional
✓ You don't have to attend the Friday call — it is optional. (no obligation)
✗ You must not come early. → means "it is forbidden to come early"!
✓ You don't have to come early. → means "it is not necessary to come early"

// MODAL QUESTIONS — POLITE & PROFESSIONAL

Could you + base verb? → polite request
✓ Could you send me the updated project timeline by Thursday?
Should we + base verb? → asking for advice/decision
✓ Should we schedule the Romania visit for May or June?
Can / Could I + base verb? → asking for permission politely
✓ Could I present this to the board next Friday?

// CERTAINTY SCALE — POSSIBILITY MODALS

100% certain: will / won't → The project will finish in June.
~70% likely: should / ought to → The delivery should arrive next week.
~50% possible: may / might / could → The price might increase in Q3.
0% / impossible: can't / couldn't → That can't be right — the figures don't match.
